# Consider the Opposite

## Overview

Lord, Lepper & Preston (1984, *JPSP*): telling people to "be unbiased and objective" does **nothing** — biased assimilation of evidence continues unchanged. But one instruction reliably works: "**ask yourself at each step whether you would have made the same evaluation had exactly the same study produced results on the other side of the issue.**" Generating the opposite case isn't a virtue exercise; it mechanically forces retrieval of evidence and interpretations your confirmation-driven search skipped (Wason, 1960: people test hypotheses by seeking confirming instances by default).

**Objectivity is not a stance you adopt; it's a specific counterfactual you compute.**

## When to Use

- Every source you check agrees with you (search-term bias: you searched for the confirmation)
- Interpreting ambiguous evidence — logs, user feedback, benchmark results — that "fits"
- A diagnosis felt right immediately and subsequent checks kept "verifying" it
- Evaluating a study/article/benchmark whose conclusion you like

## The Procedure

1. **The swap test**: same evidence, opposite conclusion — would you accept the methodology then? If you'd reject the method when it disagrees with you, reject it now.
2. **Argue the reverse for real**: write 3 concrete reasons the opposite of your belief could be true. Fewer than 3 with substance = you haven't searched, you've confirmed.
3. **Re-run the search inverted**: if you googled "why X is better than Y", now search "X problems / Y advantages". Search queries inherit your hypothesis.
4. **Explain the anomalies**: list the data points your belief explains *worst* (not best). A belief audited only against its best-fitting evidence is untested.

## Quick Reference

| Symptom | Counter-move |
|---|---|
| All evidence agrees | Invert the search query |
| Explanation felt right instantly | 3 substantive reasons for the opposite |
| Methodology looks fine (and agrees with you) | Swap test |
| "The data clearly shows…" | Which data fits worst? |

## Common Mistakes

- Generating strawman opposites that are easy to dismiss — the opposite case must be the one a smart opponent would make (see blind-steelman).
- Considering the opposite *after* committing publicly — do it while the belief is still cheap to change.
- Confusing this with indecision. You still conclude; you just conclude after the counterfactual, not instead of it.
