# Unknown Unknowns

## Overview

The knowledge matrix (1960s aerospace/defense risk practice — "unk-unks", documented from 1968, common at NASA; made famous by Rumsfeld's February 2002 briefing, who credited NASA's William Graham; the Johari Window of Luft & Ingham, 1955, is an analogous, independently developed 2×2):

| | We know it | We don't know it |
|---|---|---|
| **We know we know/don't** | Known knowns (facts) | Known unknowns (open questions) |
| **We don't know we…** | Unknown knowns (unexamined assumptions, tacit knowledge) | **Unknown unknowns** (unmodeled surprises) |

Risk registers only ever contain known unknowns — by definition. The two bottom-row cells are where projects die: **unknown knowns** (assumptions so deep nobody wrote them down) and **unknown unknowns** (categories you haven't imagined). Neither yields to direct listing; both yield to *indirect* hunting.

## When to Use

- Risk lists that look complete (they're complete over the top row only)
- Novel work with no precedent in your own history
- Post-planning, pre-commitment ("what category of thing haven't we considered?")
- After a surprise: classify which cell it came from to fix the process, not just the instance

## The Procedure — hunting each cell

1. **Known unknowns**: list open questions; assign each a resolution action and date. Cheap; do it fully.
2. **Unknown knowns** (assumption excavation): complete the sentence "this plan works as long as ___" ten times. The last three are the load-bearing tacit ones. Also: explain the plan to an outsider — everything you have to explain that "goes without saying" is an unknown known surfacing.
3. **Unknown unknowns** (indirect only):
   - **Reference-class scan**: what surprised OTHER people doing similar work? Their unknown unknowns can become your known ones for free (see blind-outside-view).
   - **Category sweep**: walk a fixed taxonomy — people, dependencies, legal, security, data, timing, money, environment — asking "what haven't I even thought about here?" A checklist can't contain your surprise, but it can point your attention at unlit areas.
   - **Fresh eyes**: someone outside the plan reviews it cold — outsiders don't share your selection filter (see blind-johari-window).
   - **Buffer for the residual**: whatever survives the hunt, budget explicitly for surprise (Flyvbjerg: uplifts, not optimism).

## Common Mistakes

- Claiming a complete risk register. The correct closing line is "plus a residual we cannot enumerate, buffered by X."
- Trying to brainstorm unknown unknowns directly — five more minutes of the same mind produces the same categories. Change the *mind* (outsider) or the *data* (reference class), not the effort.
- Ignoring unknown knowns because unknown unknowns sound scarier. Post-mortems attribute far more failures to "we all assumed…" than to true black swans.
